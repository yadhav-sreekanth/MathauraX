Game Theory

Game Theory studies strategic interaction between rational decision-makers. It analyzes how players choose strategies to maximize their payoff, considering other players’ choices.

Basic Concepts

Players

The decision-makers in a game, each aiming to maximize their own payoff.

Strategies

A complete plan of action a player may take in all possible situations within the game.

Payoffs

The rewards or outcomes players receive depending on the strategies chosen by all players.

Types of Games

  • Cooperative vs Non-Cooperative: Whether players can form binding agreements.
  • Zero-Sum vs Non-Zero-Sum: Whether one player’s gain is exactly another’s loss.
  • Simultaneous vs Sequential: Whether players move at the same time or in turns.

Example 1: Consider the classic Prisoner’s Dilemma, where two players choose to either Cooperate or Defect.

Payoff matrix:

  • If both cooperate, each gets 3 points.
  • If one defects while the other cooperates, the defector gets 5, cooperator gets 0.
  • If both defect, both get 1 point.

Step-by-Step: Finding Nash Equilibrium

  1. List the players and their possible strategies.
  2. Construct the payoff matrix showing payoffs for all strategy combinations.
  3. For each player, identify best responses to other players’ strategies.
  4. Find strategy profiles where no player can improve payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy — these are Nash equilibria.

Example 2: Find the Nash equilibrium in the Prisoner’s Dilemma described above.

Solution: Both players defecting (Defect, Defect) is the Nash equilibrium since neither can improve payoff by changing strategy alone.

Practice Problems

1. Two firms compete by choosing quantities to produce (Cournot competition). Firm A chooses 10 units, Firm B chooses 20 units. Discuss what strategy Firm A should adopt if Firm B’s choice is fixed.

Solution:

Firm A should produce the quantity that maximizes its profit given Firm B’s quantity (best response function). Generally, it will choose less or more based on market demand and cost functions.

2. In the Prisoner’s Dilemma, what happens if the game is repeated multiple times? How might strategies change?

Solution:

Repeated games allow strategies like Tit-for-Tat, encouraging cooperation through punishment and reward over time, potentially leading to cooperation equilibria.

3. Identify whether the following game is zero-sum: Player 1 gains 4 points, Player 2 loses 3 points.

Solution:

No, because Player 1’s gain (4) does not exactly equal Player 2’s loss (3). Hence, it’s non-zero-sum.

4. Explain the difference between pure and mixed strategies with an example.

Solution:

Pure strategy: A player chooses one action deterministically (e.g., always Cooperate).

Mixed strategy: A player randomizes over possible actions with certain probabilities (e.g., Cooperate with 70%, Defect with 30%).
