Combinatorics Basics

Combinatorics is the branch of mathematics dealing with counting, arrangement, and selection of objects. It helps solve problems related to how many ways things can happen in an organized manner.

Key Concepts in Combinatorics

Factorial (n!)

The factorial of a non-negative integer n is the product of all positive integers less than or equal to n.

Example: 5! = 5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 120

Permutation

A permutation is an arrangement of objects in a specific order.

Formula for permutations of n distinct objects taken r at a time:

P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}

Permutation with Repetition

If some objects are repeated, the total permutations are divided by factorials of the counts of repeated objects.

Formula for n objects with repetitions: n_1, n_2, ..., n_k repeated:

\frac{n!}{n_1! \times n_2! \times \cdots \times n_k!}

Circular Permutations

Number of ways to arrange n objects around a circle:

(n - 1)!

Because rotations are considered the same arrangement.

Combination

A combination is a selection of objects without regard to order.

Formula for combinations of n distinct objects taken r at a time:

C(n, r) = \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}

Combination with Repetition

Number of ways to choose r objects from n types allowing repetition:

C(n + r - 1, r) = \binom{n + r - 1}{r} = \frac{(n+r-1)!}{r!(n-1)!}

Binomial Theorem

The binomial theorem describes the algebraic expansion of powers of a binomial:

(a + b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k

This links binomial coefficients (combinations) with algebra.

Fundamental Counting Principle

If one event can happen in m ways and another independent event in n ways, total ways both happen is:

m \times n

Pigeonhole Principle

If n items are placed into k boxes and n > k, then at least one box contains more than one item.

Inclusion-Exclusion Principle

Used to count the number of elements in the union of overlapping sets.

For two sets A and B:

|A \cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A \cap B|

Extends similarly for more sets.

Multinomial Coefficients

Generalization of combinations for splitting n items into groups of sizes n_1, n_2, ..., n_k (where n_1 + n_2 + ... + n_k = n):

\binom{n}{n_1, n_2, ..., n_k} = \frac{n!}{n_1! \times n_2! \times \cdots \times n_k!}

Examples & Step-by-Step Solutions

Example 1: Permutations

How many ways to arrange 5 different books on a shelf?

  1. Number of objects: 5
  2. Order matters, so use permutations formula: 5! = 120
  3. Therefore, 120 ways to arrange.

Example 2: Permutations with Repetition

How many ways to arrange the letters in the word "BALLOON"?

  1. Total letters: 7
  2. Repeated letters: L(2), O(2)
  3. Formula: \frac{7!}{2! \times 2!} = \frac{5040}{4} = 1260
  4. So, 1260 unique arrangements.

Example 3: Combinations

From 10 students, how many ways to select a committee of 3?

  1. Total students: 10
  2. Committee size: 3
  3. Use combinations formula: \binom{10}{3} = \frac{10!}{3!7!} = 120
  4. Answer: 120 ways.

Example 4: Combination with Repetition

How many ways to choose 4 ice cream scoops from 3 flavors if repetition allowed?

  1. Flavors: 3, Scoops: 4
  2. Use formula: \binom{3+4-1}{4} = \binom{6}{4} = 15
  3. So, 15 ways.

Example 5: Circular Permutations

Number of ways to seat 6 people around a round table?

  1. Total people: 6
  2. Use circular permutation formula: (6 - 1)! = 5! = 120
  3. Answer: 120 ways.

Example 6: Pigeonhole Principle

If 13 people are in a room, prove that at least two share the same birth month.

  1. Months = 12
  2. People = 13 > 12
  3. By pigeonhole principle, at least one month must have 2 or more people.

Example 7: Inclusion-Exclusion

In a class, 20 students study Math, 15 study Physics, and 5 study both. How many study at least one?

  1. Total studying Math or Physics: |M \cup P| = |M| + |P| - |M \cap P|
  2. = 20 + 15 - 5 = 30
  3. Answer: 30 students.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: How many ways to arrange the letters in "STATISTICS"?

Word length: 10 letters

Repeated letters: S(3), T(3), I(2)

Permutations = \frac{10!}{3! \times 3! \times 2!} = 50400

Problem 2: From 12 different books, how many ways to select 5?

Use combinations: \binom{12}{5} = 792

Problem 3: How many different 3-digit numbers can be formed using digits 1-5 without repetition?

Number of digits: 5

Choosing 3 digits and arranging in order: P(5, 3) = \frac{5!}{(5-3)!} = 60

Problem 4: How many ways to seat 4 people around a round table?

Circular permutations: (4 - 1)! = 3! = 6

Problem 5: If you toss 3 coins, how many possible outcomes are there?

Each coin has 2 outcomes, so total: 2^3 = 8
